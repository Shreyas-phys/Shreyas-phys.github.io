Problem 1: Quantum Cryptography

Describe the key principles of quantum cryptography and how it ensures secure communication in the presence of quantum attacks.

Solution:

Quantum cryptography utilizes principles like superposition and entanglement to create unbreakable encryption methods. In particular, the BB84 protocol uses quantum states to securely share keys. The security of this method arises from the fact that any attempt to eavesdrop on the quantum state will inevitably disturb it, making the attack detectable.

The security of quantum key distribution is mathematically proven using the no-cloning theorem and Heisenberg’s uncertainty principle: $$ \Delta x \cdot \Delta p \geq \frac{\hbar}{2} $$ where \( x \) is the position and \( p \) is the momentum, indicating the inherent uncertainty in quantum measurements.

Problem 2: Quantum Computing Algorithms

Explain how quantum algorithms differ from classical ones and provide an example of a quantum algorithm.

Solution:

Quantum algorithms leverage quantum parallelism, superposition, and entanglement to perform computations more efficiently than classical algorithms. A classical computer must process each possibility sequentially, while a quantum computer can process many possibilities simultaneously due to superposition.

One well-known quantum algorithm is Shor's algorithm, which can factor large integers exponentially faster than the best-known classical algorithms. The time complexity of classical factoring is superpolynomial, whereas Shor’s algorithm runs in polynomial time: $$ T = O\left( (\log N)^3 \right) $$ where \( N \) is the integer to be factored.

Another quantum algorithm is Grover's search algorithm, which solves unstructured search problems. It can search through an unsorted database of \( N \) items in \( O(\sqrt{N}) \) steps, as opposed to the classical \( O(N) \) steps: $$ T_{\text{Grover}} = O(\sqrt{N}) $$
